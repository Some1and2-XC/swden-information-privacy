Introduction

Sweden, renowned for its progressive policies and high standard of living, offers a fascinating case study in ethical governance, legislative practices, and cultural dynamics. In exploring various facets of Swedish society, ranging from healthcare management to data privacy laws and political activism, we gain valuable insights into the nation's values, priorities, and unique approach to governance.

At the heart of Sweden's ethical framework lies a commitment to principles such as freedom of choice and equity in healthcare access. These principles are exemplified in the country's healthcare system, where patients are empowered to choose their care providers, and resources are allocated based on individual preferences. However, recent studies have shed light on potential shortcomings in the implementation of ethical values within healthcare management, prompting deeper reflections on the intersection of policy and ethics.

Legislatively, Sweden navigates its position within the European Union while upholding its own national standards. Compliance with EU regulations, particularly in the realm of data protection, underscores Sweden's dedication to safeguarding individual privacy rights. The Swedish Authority for Privacy Protection (IMY) plays a pivotal role in enforcing the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), ensuring accountability and transparency in data handling practices.

Culturally, Sweden's progressive values are reflected in its political landscape and societal norms. The prominence of women in the Riksdag and the country's high rankings in social progress indices attest to Sweden's commitment to inclusivity and social welfare. Moreover, grassroots movements, such as the Swedish Pirate Party, advocate for digital rights and challenge traditional paradigms, showcasing a vibrant culture of activism and civic engagement.

This exploration of Sweden's ethical, legislative, and cultural landscape offers valuable insights into the nation's identity and governance philosophy. By delving into the intricacies of Swedish society, we gain a deeper understanding of the factors that contribute to its reputation as a unique and progressive nation on the global stage.

Ethical Practices

To get an understanding of the ethical practices of the Swedish government it is important to look at how the swedish government handles its policies around its own initiatives. One good example is healthcare.

In 2010, there was a reform to the Swedish healthcare system which takes inspiration from the free market and was based on three principles.

  • "freedom-of-choice"
  • "any-qualified-provider"
  • "money-follows-the-patient-principle"

The first principle, "freedom-of-choice" refers to the attitude that patients have the right to choose freely which primary care center they prefer. The purpose of this is to give more power to the patient in how they want to be treated.

The second principle, "any-qualitfied-provider" was inspired by the open market and this principle gives private organizations the ability to compete for public reimbursements.

The third principle, "money-follows-the-patient-principle" indicates the patients choices should impact the allocation of resources.

The thing that all three of these reforms have in common is that they give more choice to the Swedish people. They also are a reflection of the Swedish government putting policies in place to make sure that the taxes that are received go towards things that improve the lives of the people.

While this is an example of how the Swedish government allocates resources to help its people (as Sweden is a democracy this is expected,) it is also important to take a closer look at what studies made specifically for looking at the ethics of Sweden have to say.

The status of ethics in Swedish health care management: a qualitative study

Here is the contents of the 'background' section of this study. The background of the study states:

"
By tradition, the Swedish health care system is based on a representative and parliamentary form of government. Recently, new management forms, inspired by market principles, have developed. The steering system is both national and regional, in that self-governing county councils are responsible for the financing and provision of health care in different regions. National and local documents regulating Swedish health care mention several ethical values, such as equity in health for the whole population and respect for autonomy and human dignity. It is therefore of interest to investigate the status of such ethical statements in Swedish health care management.
" (Höglund & Falkenström, 2018, Abstract)

To summarize, this study will look at how the Swedish healthcare industry's treatment of its citizens in practice aligns with the ethics of values of the Swedish government. The following sections are going to cover some of this study's findings.

Of course, as this is a quote from a politician it is important to have the understanding that they can potentially benefit from having a study corroborate things they campaign for.

This quote seems as if the speaker is disapointed in how the health care act turned out. This can indicate that from this politicians perspective, they could have done more to ensure that Swedens policies always have the patient in mind when being decided upon.

Cost Effectiveness over Ethics

One theme the study found was that the people the study interviewed had the perspective that the economy and KPIs such as the amount of hospital visits and volumes of supplies didn't always favor the patient. The study implies that the KPIs and metrics that get used the most often have very little to do with ethics.

Key takeaways

While the study points out various aspects of the Swedish healthcare system that need improvement, the fact that there is investigation into how well parts of its government operates shows transparency which can't be overlooked.

It should also be noted that while the study does showcase the downsides of having healthcare's private and public sector compete, it is important to remember that the purpose of the original reforms seems to be an attempt at making the budget for healthcare more effective.

Legislative Practices

EU & Article 8

Because Sweden is a member of the EU, many laws in regard to data privacy and information is simply the same as the rest of the European Union. This section is going to go over some of the laws the EU has in place for its member states.

The European Union has the ‚ÄúCharter of Fundamental Rights of the European Union.‚Äù Title III Article 8 from their charter of rights called ‚ÄúProtection of personal data‚Äù says the following:

"
Article 8
Protection of personal data
1. Everyone has the right to the protection of personal data concerning him or her.
2. Such data must be processed fairly for specified purposes and on the basis of the consent of the person concerned or some other legitimate basis laid down by law. Everyone has the right of access to data which has been collected concerning him or her, and the right to have it rectified.
3. Compliance with these rules shall be subject to control by an independent authority.
"

This is put in place to guarantee the rights of the citizens living in the EU's member states. This list of rights are very permissive for the rights to privacy for individuals especially in the modern day.

IMY & GDPR

For Sweden, the 'independent authority' which is required by article 8 is the IMY. The IMY (which stands for Swedish Authority for Privacy Protection, its a swedish acronym.) The IMY in Sweden is responsible for ensuring individuals personal privacy is being respected. The organization is also responsible for guiding organizations to comply with the GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation.) The IMY has the authority to place expensive fines on organizations for breaching its policies. Here is an example of the IMY going after google for a breach of their data governance laws.

"
Gothenburg Administrative Court of Appeal, case nr 2232-21. In this case, Google LLC was fined for not fulfilling its obligations in respect of the right to request delisting (right to be forgotten under the GDPR). The Court affirmed the fine set by the Stockholm Administrative Court, but lowered it from SEK 52 million (approx. ‚Ç¨4.46 million) to SEK 50 million (approx. ‚Ç¨4.29 million). The fine was lowered as the Court ruled that Google had not violated any provisions of the GDPR with reference to the individual complaints, but only with reference to its internal procedures. Google appealed the decision, but on 20 December 2022, the Supreme Administrative Court decided not to grant leave to appeal;
"

While the fine was lowered, to SEK 50 million (around CAD 6.3 million,) this still shows how the IMY does take steps to try and enforce their information privacy laws to ensure the privacy of its citizens.

Riksdag

The Riksdag is the Swedish parliament. This is required as Sweden is a parliamentary democracy. The purpose of the Riksdag is to make decisions about laws to be put in place as well as to ammend the Swedish constitution when needed.

Piratbyrån

The Swedish pirate party is a political party founded in 2006. From a web snapshot taken in 2009 from archive.org, their original website had 3 issues it was fighting for.

#1 Reform copyright law

In this section, the pirate party argues that copyright laws restrict the thing that they were trying to promote. They argue that the system is outdated and that it isn‚Äôt fair that there is "no point in restoring balance and reason to the legislation, if at the same time we continue to allow the big media companies to both write and enforce their own arbitrary laws."

#2 An abolished patent system

In this section they argue that pharmaceutical patents are extremely harmful and get in the way of helping people. The pirate party says that having researchers lock up their findings in a patent causes people to die because their research isn‚Äôt freely available.

#3 Respect the right to privacy

This section states that Europeans should be aware of the horrific consequences of not taking privacy seriously. It states that "We must pull the emergency brake on the runaway train towards a society we do not want," meaning the mass surveillance done by the state must be stopped and that there is dire consequences of not acting.

Summary

The current version of their website is far more toned down with some revisions. In the Reform copyright law section it says this ‚ÄúThe non-profit copyright must remain unchanged.‚Äù It also adds that it wants to reform library lending. They state they want to remove lending caps and DRM on E-books.

Outrage over a new copyright law and convictions over the four founders of the pirate bay in 2009 caused a surge in popularity for the niche pirate party causing its size to more than double, winning 7.1% of the votes in Sweden and one of Swedens 18 seats in the European Unions parliament. While the founders of the pirate bay were convicted, in Sweden if you appeal a conviction you don't get incarcerated until after the appeal process. Further more according to an interview with Peter Sunde a co-founder of the pirate bay, in Sweden you aren‚Äôt required to assist with getting incarcerated as that is the job of the state.

"
Interesting thing in Sweden is that it is not illegal to not go to prison, so when you get sentenced to prison, I think this is actually the case in most countries. Is that you're not the ones getting sentenced. You can‚Äôt force someone to go to prison. There is no human emotion that says I want to be locked in. So in Sweden at least, it the state that gets sentenced to put you in prison, not you as a person. The state has the responsibility of locking you up, you don‚Äôt have to help whatsoever. Which also means its not illegal in Sweden to escape from prison. Because its a human trait that you don‚Äôt want to be locked up
"

In Sweden, it seems as though many parts of its legislation center around natural human behavior. Because of quirks such as these the pirate bay, while still constantly being threatened by DMCAs and American copyright holders (with lots of effort) can still exist to this day.

Conclusion

A part of the culture of the Sweden population is to respect personal privacy and to respect human nature. Because of this, certain things can happen that seems impossible to do in most other places in the world. Not helping with the state to lock you up and even escaping from prison carrying no penalty at all while at the same time giving out multi million dollar fines to companies that don't allow users to remove their information is something that doesn't seem possible in the same country.

Many of the laws regarding data security are taken very seriously and have steep penalties for not following guidelines. The Swedish government also works very well and this is shown by the effectiveness of its healthcare system. This is what makes Sweden a terribly unique and interesting country.

In conclusion, the exploration of Sweden's ethical, legislative, and cultural landscape offers valuable insights into the nation's identity and governance philosophy. By navigating the complexities of Swedish society, we gain a deeper appreciation for its unique blend of progressive values, legislative frameworks, and civic activism, which collectively contribute to its reputation as a global leader in ethical governance and societal well-being.
